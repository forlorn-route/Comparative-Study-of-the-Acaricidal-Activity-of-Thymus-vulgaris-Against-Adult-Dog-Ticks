Comparative Study of the Acaricidal Activity of Thymus vulgaris Against Adult Dog Tick (Rhipicephalus sanguineus)

Authors: Zohreh Alibeigi; Ehsan Rakhshandehroo; Mohmmad Jamal Saharkhiz; Amir Mootabi Alavi September 2021
Jump to: Abstract, Introduction, Materials and Methods, Results, Discussion, Conclusion, References

Abstract

Ticks are among the most significant ectoparasites affecting domestic animals and are responsible for transmitting numerous pathogens, with Rhipicephalus sanguineus being one of the most widely distributed species worldwide (Dantas-Torres, 2008; George et al., 2004). The increasing resistance of ticks to synthetic acaricides, coupled with environmental and health concerns, has intensified the search for plant-based alternatives (Isman, 2006; Quadros et al., 2020). This study evaluated the acaricidal activity of aqueous Thymus vulgaris leaf extracts against adult R. sanguineus using an adult immersion test (AIT). Three concentrations (50%, 75%, and 100%) were assessed, and mortality was recorded at specific time intervals. Results showed a significant, concentration-dependent increase in tick mortality, with the highest concentration exhibiting the greatest acaricidal effect. These findings support the potential of aqueous T. vulgaris extracts as a sustainable alternative to synthetic acaricides.

Keywords: Thymus vulgaris, Rhipicephalus sanguineus, acaricidal activity, aqueous extract, adult immersion test

Introduction

Ticks are obligate blood-feeding ectoparasites that pose serious threats to animal health by causing blood loss, dermatitis, anemia, and by transmitting bacterial, viral, and protozoan pathogens (Jongejan & Uilenberg, 2004; Walker et al., 2014). The brown dog tick, Rhipicephalus sanguineus, is of particular concern due to its cosmopolitan distribution and its ability to infest dogs in both rural and urban environments (Dantas-Torres, 2008; Estrada-Peña et al., 2017). Tick control relies heavily on synthetic acaricides; however, prolonged and indiscriminate use has led to the development of resistance, reduced efficacy, and contamination of the environment (George et al., 2004; Abbas et al., 2014). Additionally, chemical residues may pose risks to non-target organisms, animals, and humans (Benelli et al., 2016). As a result, research has increasingly focused on botanical acaricides, which. biodegradable, environmentally friendly, and often locally available (Isman, 2006; Regnault-Roger et al., 2012). Thymus vulgaris is a medicinal plant known to contain bioactive compounds such as flavonoid and phenol, which exhibit insecticidal and acaricidal properties (Bakkali et al., 2008; Alibeigi et al., 2021). While essential leaf extract of T. vulgaris have been extensively studied, aqueous extracts remain less explored despite being safer and more accessible. This study therefore aimed to evaluate the acaricidal activity of aqueous T. vulgaris>\ leaf extracts against adult R. sanguineus and to determine the most effective concentration using a laboratory-based bioassay.

Materials and Methods

Ethical Approval

All experimental procedures were conducted following institutional laboratory guidelines, and informed consent was obtained from dog owners prior to tick collection (George et al., 2004; Walker et al., 2014).

Plant Material

Fresh Thymus vulgaris leaves were collected. The leaves were washed, shade-dried, and ground into fine powder following standard phytochemical preparation protocols (Harborne, 1998; Isman, 2006).

Preparation of Extracts

Powdered leaves were macerated in distilled water for 18–24 hours with occasional agitation, filtered, and stored as the 100% aqueous extract. Dilutions of 50%, and 75% were prepared following methods described by Politi et al. (2012) and Zorloni et al. (2010).

Collection of Ticks

Adult unfed R. sanguineus ticks were manually collected from naturally infested dogs and maintained under controlled laboratory conditions prior to testing (Dantas-Torres, 2008; Jongejan & Uilenberg, 2004).

Adult Immersion Test (AIT)

The ticks were randomly assigned to treatment groups. Each concentration (50%, 75%, 100%) and the Amitraz as the positive control were tested with 8 replicates, each replicate consisting of 10 adult ticks. Ticks in each replicate were uniformly sprayed with their assigned extract concentration or control solution for two minutes following a modified adult contact test protocol based on Drummond et al. (1973) and adapted by Politi et al. (2012). Mortality was observed and recorded at 2, 6, 12, and 24 hours post-treatment. Ticks that showed no movement or response to gentle stimulation were considered dead.

Statistical Analysis

Observed mortality was corrected using Abbott’s formula (Abbott, 1925). Data were analyzed using one-way analysis of variance (ANOVA), followed by Tukey’s post-hoc test to determine significant differences among treatments (p < 0.05), as recommended for acaricidal bioassays (Zorloni et al., 2010; Quadros et al., 2020).

Results

Aqueous Thymus vulgaris leaf extract produced measurable mortality in adult R. sanguineus ticks across all concentrations tested. Mortality increased significantly with increasing extract concentration, demonstrating a clear dose-dependent response. The control group exhibited notably high mortality, while the 75% and 100% concentrations resulted in the highest mean mortality. Statistical analysis confirmed significant differences among treatments (p < 0.05). Similar concentration-dependent acaricidal effects have been reported in other studies evaluating plant extracts against Rhipicephalus ticks (Politi et al., 2012; Benelli et al., 2016; Quadros et al., 2020).

Discussion

The present study demonstrates that aqueous Thymus vulgaris leaf extract possesses significant acaricidal activity against adult Rhipicephalus sanguineus, with higher concentrations producing greater mortality. This dose-dependent trend is consistent with previous studies on botanical acaricides, which attribute increased efficacy to higher levels of bioactive phytochemicals (Isman, 2006; Regnault-Roger et al., 2012). The acaricidal effect of T. vulgaris is largely associated with flavonoid and phenolic compound, which are known to disrupt arthropod nervous systems and cellular membranes (Bakkali et al., 2008; Alibeigi et al., 2021). Although essential oils often show stronger acaricidal effects, aqueous extracts remain valuable due to their safety, affordability, and ease of preparation, particularly in low-resource settings (Politi et al., 2012; Quadros et al., 2020). The findings of this study therefore support the growing body of evidence advocating the use of plant-based acaricides as sustainable alternatives to synthetic chemicals.

Conclusion

Aqueous Thymus vulgaris leaf extract exhibited significant, concentration-dependent acaricidal activity against adult Rhipicephalus sanguineus ticks under laboratory conditions. The highest concentration produced the greatest mortality, highlighting the potential of T. vulgaris as an environmentally friendly and cost-effective alternative for tick control. Further studies should focus on chemical profiling, field evaluation, and assessment of long-term safety.
